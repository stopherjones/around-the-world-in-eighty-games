    Retracing our steps somewhat we head north-east and into China - a country vast in size, population and history. 
    
    Stretching back over 4,000 years, Chinese civilisation has given the world a staggering list of inventions: paper, printing, gunpowder, the compass, silk, porcelain, and noodles, to name just a few. The Great Wall stretches thousands of kilometres across mountains and desert. At its height, the Chinese Empire was the largest economy on earth by a considerable margin, a position it looks increasingly likely to reclaim in the 21st century. 
    
    But China is not just a story of power and invention, it is a civilisation built on profound philosophical and spiritual foundations. Confucianism shaped Chinese society for millennia, placing family loyalty, respect for elders, education, and moral duty at the heart of everyday life. Taoism added a philosophy of harmony and balance -  of yin and yang - and Buddhism was embraced so thoroughly that China became one of its great heartlands, filling the landscape with monasteries, pagodas, and golden Buddhas.

    Few things are more iconically Chinese than the giant panda, something which provides the thematic basis for our first game, Takenoko. In Takenoko, you are a gardener in the Emperor's palace, tasked with cultivating a bamboo garden and keeping the Imperial panda fed and happy. Players take turns growing bamboo, redirecting water, and laying out garden tiles, all while trying to satisfy their own secret objectives. It's a beautiful, gentle game yet with enough competition bubbling beneath the surface to keep everyone on their toes. Think of it as the calm before the storm, because our next game is much more demanding...
    
    Our second game, In the Year of the Dragon is an unforgiving game requiring precise strategic planning. Set in Imperial China against a backdrop of calamity, plagues, droughts, Mongol invasions, and firework celebrations all descend on your dynasty in turn. Your job is to survive them all with your palace intact. You'll be recruiting advisors, managing rice, collecting tribute, and making increasingly painful decisions about who and what to sacrifice when disaster strikes.
